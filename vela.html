Background

Context

Water sport enthusiasts such as surfers, kayakers, and snorkelers want their activities on the water to be relaxing and fun, not stressful. To avoid any mishaps, it’s important to check the winds, waves and weather conditions before heading out. This can help us avoid hazards and be fully prepared for a day full of surfing, diving, or sailing.

Problem

Weather apps do exist for water sport enthusiasts, however what is currently available doesn’t do a great job of displaying complicated weather data in an accessible, easy to understand manner.

Hypothesis

Watersport enthusiasts such as sailors, surfers, and divers need a way to receive accurate weather data presented in an easy to comprehend and visually attractive way, so that they can make informed decisions about participating in water sports.

Goal

Create a product that offers current conditions information, graphs, and future forecasts all beautifully displayed and easy to read at a glance, geared specifically for the needs of water sport enthusiasts.

Research

Competitive Analysis

As part of my research, it was important to understand competitors in the same space were doing well, and discover what opportunities might exist for my project. I selected two apps with similar features, Waterspeed and Windy. To analyze, I created a marketing profile for each competitor, prepared a SWOT analysis, and performed a UX and features analysis of each competitor.

Usability Testing

Usability testing was conducted remotely with six participants over the span of the week. During the test we had the participants interact with the Prototype and complete a series of short tasks. Our test objectives were to determine how quickly users were able to understand what the features of the app do, and also we wanted to observe how users would navigate through the app and if they're able to find what they needed quickly and easily through the navigation.

View full test plan and script

Test Results

The top three usability issues that we discovered during testing were:

  • Users were confused on where to find the ocean swell forecast. Users anticipated that ocean swell would be located under the “water” bucket, when in fact the data was placed under the “Air” bucket, as swell is caused by wind.
  • Users were confused by the search function when asked to set their location in the app. This was because the search function prototype did not indicate clearly where the user could type.
  • When users created a weather alert, there was confusion because there was no clear indiction when the user had completed the task. In this instance, the toggle that indicated an alert had been created did not move to the “on” position.

Refining the Design

After usability testing was completed, I sat down with the high fidelity wireframes to further refine the design. I incorporated the fixes suggested by testing, including changing the location of certain data, and making some changes to the search function to improve usability. I also implemented an 8 point grid system, added a color scheme, and adapted the interface to include Material Design Guidelines. An accessibility audit was also performed to make sure the design conformed to A guidelines at a minimum. Finally, I created a Style Guide in preparation for handoff to developers.

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Research - Finding the right people to interview

During the research phase of Vela it was challenging it was difficult to find individuals in my personal network who had water sports experience and matched my criteria, and it was also sometimes felt challenging to ask for the interview if I had not spoken with the contact in a long time. There were also some scheduling challenges with individuals who lived in different time zones. The lesson learned here is to over budget time for research to allow for any cancellations or scheduling mishaps. I overcame the challenge of sourcing individuals with persistence and not allowing myself to become discouraged if an interview did not work out. I was careful to follow up when setting up the interview and carefully explained the process to ensure the participant’s comfort.

Design - Receiving Feedback

While designing the first concept of the design, I received some feedback that the navigation I had created may not be the easiest thing for a developer to build. Because of this, I had to rethink and come up with a new concept. The lesson learned for me here was that it is 1) Design is a constant work in progress and it’s important not to get attached to any one idea and 2) UX Design is very similar to architecture. Creating beautiful and innovative wireframes is great, but ultimately functionality is most important and someone has to be able to build the design.

Testing - Asking better questions

An additional finding from testing the prototype was how to write better usability testing questions. For instance, during testing participants had to ask for a particular question to be repeated multiple times. I realized that the question I wrote was too long, involved, and wordy. Participants could not remember what the question was asking them to do as they tried to complete the task. The lesson learned here is to remember that sometimes a question seems clear to the author but not so clear to someone hearing it for the first time. I learned to write better questions by reading them out loud, and by running the questions by friends and family to check for clarity.
